> "Foolish child! foolish child!" was the parrot-phrase flung in answer. The waving of crooked, false-jewelled fingers gave grotesqueness to the words.

*Parrots are a fun character that authors sometimes use to highlight certain phrases either for foreshadowing or emphasis.*

Reader's log analysis: The speaker of the quote on page 59 is a parrot. The parrot interjects with this short exclamation in response to Sibyl deciding to leave Mr. Isaacs for Dorian Gray. By giving this short advice though the mouth of a parrot, Wilde is able to more tightly control the perceived dynamic of Sibyl and Dorian's relationship. Wilde demonstrates Sibyl's decision to go all in on Dorian—even without knowing much about him—as a rash and unwise decision. Additionally, Wilde describes the parrot waving "crooked, false-jewelled fingers" which "gave grotesqueness to the words" (Page 59). This characterization of the parent enunciates the advice as bizarre, signifying the lack of foresight from the Vane family of the danger that is to come; they hear these warnings as weird and unusual. Through the action of portraying Sibyl's tendency to solely act based on emotions, Wilde furthers his message that human relationships are necessary and important in a person's life when they are created slowly and carefully.

**Beginning Question:** As Dorian becomes more and more evil, Dorian works to convince himself that he did nothing wrong, even going as far as to tell himself in regard to Sibyl's death, "Dorian Gray had not killed her." How do quotes like these display Dorian's adolescent characteristics and what effect do these characteristics have on Wilde's overall message?

**Think Aloud:** The quote "Dorian Gray had not killed her" (Page 120) displays Dorian's inability to control and manage his emotions, a common characteristic of young adults. In addition to Dorian's physical characteristics of not being able to age and get more mature, Dorian's brain is hindered by curse as well. Moreover, Lord Henry takes advantage of Dorian's vulnerability in this regard by influencing Dorian's thoughts with a story which Dorian believes holds all that he needs to know on how to live his life. By including this character who takes advantage of Dorian's not-yet-mature brain, Wilde demonstrates the fragility of the human mind under stress and the ease of which nefarious people can manipulate and corrupt them into making bad long term decisions.
