## Introduction

January 2, 1948, Steinbeck wrote “I am going to start a long book this summer and I want to find out many things first”. For the next five years, he would research and write _East of Eden_. During three major life events for Steinbeck happened: the breakup of his second marriage with Gwyn, his departure from California to New York, and the death of his friend and mentor Ed Ricketts. (ix)

The book is written to his sons, one is six and the other is eight (xiv). His first wife was Elaine (x). Steinbeck loves places.

“Steinbeck’s worldly analysis of prostitution coincides with his romanticization of it and women as a group” (xvii).

## Part 1 - Chapter 1

Setting: Salinas Valley in Northern California. Mountains, rivers

> I always found in myself a dread of west and a love of east. (3)

As mentioned in the introduction, this has to do with him eventually leaving California to New York.

> willows straightened up with the flood debris in their upper branches (4)

Very vivid descriptions about the environment. The imagery here seems to suggest this place in California is perfect, or Steinbeck’s version of perfect.

> Once, fifty miles down the valley, my father bored a well (4)

Ok, some more basic character details. It is significant that the father did this, possibly suggesting that the father was into philanthropy and contributing to his community.

> rich years when the rainfall was plentiful. But there were dry years too (5)

After a lot more imagery of how beautiful this farm with flowers and animals we learn the beauty does depend on the weather. Also, the narrator mentions his grandfather could come to this farm as well. The rain would come for five of six years and then leave which could cause “cows [to] grow thin and sometimes starve to death” (5). People could sell their things and move away, then when the water comes, they would forget everything and go back to enjoying. _Live in the moment?_

They go on to describe the history of the Salinas Valley

> First there were Indians, an inferior breed without energy, inventiveness, or culture (6)

That is… a slight biased perspective on Native Americans.

> Then the Americans came … They took the lands, remade the laws to make their titles good (7)

The narrator describes how farmland quickly took over the lands and how people would center wherever there was water for good soil. They would also name places based on people.

Reader’s log analysis: Throughout the book, East of Eden repeatedly brings up bad parenting and the sociological effects these mistakes creates on the children. When Adam was born, Cyrus was distracted by his wife committing suicide, and thus, he failed to provide the necessary attention to his newly born child. The narrator describes that "the baby was drunk for two days and a half" and that Cyrus had even "neglected to feed him" (16). Cyrus intoxicates Adam so that he does not cry. This is not the only time characters in the book sacrifice their long term health for short term gains. Adam himself also tries many times to hide from his problems and emotions by using alcohol and other distractions. Namely, when Adam is discharged from the military the second time, Adam does not have the drive to find his purpose so he mindlessly wanders the streets and gets caught up in trouble. Both cases demonstrate how without a goal and clear direction to get there, characters make decisions that end up being a detriment in the future.

Reader’s log analysis: Adam discusses with Charles how he feels to Cyrus. Steinbeck redefines the meaning of love when Adam thinks, "Maybe if I had loved him I would have been jealous of him. You were" (70). This revelation ties into the biblical Cain and Abel allegory that carries throughout the entire book. Steinbeck suggests that true love ties to jealousy. Adam does not have the same jealousy for Cyrus as Charles. Charles, on the other hand, is the Cain figure whose violence does not come from evil, but rejected love and jealousy. Overall, the connection of love and jealousy demonstrates how those who love the most also suffer the most when that love is broken.

As Charles is making all these conclusions about his father, Adam doesn’t believe he stole. Charles is meanwhile going through an existential crisis, his whole life he has viewed Cyrus as this father he wishes cared for him more. Adam is the opposite which is why Charles cares very much, but Adam is just accepting the money without all the conclusions. “You were fighting for your love” (71).
