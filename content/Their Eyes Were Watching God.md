## Chapter 2

Janie tells her life story. Her grandmother raised her whom she called Nanny, and they lived in the backyard of a white family's house. This was West Florida, the white family was the Washburns. (8).

> Ah was wid dem white chillun so much till Ah didn't know Ah wuzn't white till Ah was round six years old. Wouldn't have found it out then, but a man come long takin' pictures and without askin' anybody (8).

They were around white people so much Janie thought she was also white until she saw a photograph of herself. *I feel like there are other ways of looking at yourself but more power to you, I guess, never being interested in what you look like.*

They all laughed at her when she couldn't find herself in the picture. They called her Alphabet because people have named to so many names. (9).

The kids at her black school would pick on her because she wasn't fully black. Bloodhounds. (9).

Nanny bought land and Ms Washburn helped take their stuff to "Nanny's gate" (10).

Nanny saw Jane let **Johnny Taylor** kiss her at that gate. The rest of the day she sat under a "blossoming pear tree" *Holy symbolism*. (10). We get very sexually charged imagery of the tree and nature. She sees a bee be hugged by a flower, "So this was a marriage!" (11).

## Chapter 3

Janie questions if she loves Logan and decides that she would love him "the old folks had said it, so it must be so" (21). I think she trusts them too much but at the same time I am happy she is willing to put in effort to try to make this work so that her life won't be miserable.

They got married on a Saturday evening "with three cakes" and lots to eat (21).

> But anyhow Janie went on inside to wait for love to begin. The new moon had been up and down three times before she got worried in mind (22).

Things are not falling into place.

> Ah wants things sweet wid mah marriage lak when you sit under a pear tree and think. Ah. . . . (24)

Janie tells Nanny that she isn't feeling the sweetness of love, but Nanny downplays her concerts telling her that she should be grateful that he is rich and has 60 acres of land. "Wait awhile, baby. Yo' mind will change" (24). This constant downplaying her own thoughts is going to prevent Janie from thinking independently, and my guess is she will develop an unhealthy relationship (for herself) from this.

> She knew now that marriage did not make love. Janie's first dream was dead, so she became a woman. (25).

## Chapter 4

The chapter starts with Janie telling that even before the first year of their marriage Logan had stopped with the fake image he had put on when they first got married. He doesn't play with her hair anymore and even says his first wife chopped wood and that "You done been spoilt rotten" (26). Once again, everyone around her is downplaying Jamie's emotions and forcing her to think her wants are unreasonable and that she is crazy to think them.

Logan goes off to town to buy two mules, so Janie can help. Janie notices an attractive man, **Joe "Jody" Starks**. (28). Jody is from Georgia and wants to live in Florida.

Jody stays to rest for a few weeks, much longer than they had planned before.

> Janie pulled back a long time because he did not represent sun-up and pollen and blooming trees, but he spoke for far horizon. He spoke for change and chance. Still she hung back. The memory of Nanny was still powerful and strong. (29).

The pear tree metaphor is still going strong. Janie is intrigued by this different way of live, Jody speaks for "change and chance." But she is still influenced by the people around her.

Jody mentions Janie marrying him and then Janie asks Logan point-blank suppose I run off and leave you and Logan says that she will won't be able to take care of herself and will come back.

They fight the next morning and Logan makes some crazy threats to Janie after she refuses to follow everything he says. (31).

> The morning road air was like a new dress. That made her feel the apron tied around her waist. She untied it and flung it on a low bush beside the road and walked on, picking flowers and making a bouquet. (32)

Janie the marries Jody and runs off.

## Chapter 5

When they arrive at Florida, they meet **Lee Coker**, and he tells Janie that **Amos Hicks** is looking for someone to marry. Jody tells them that Janie is not his daughter but instead his wife, and they lose interest. (35).

The place, Eatonville, is small, only 50 acres (37) and Jody buys 200 acres in cash from Captain Eaton (38).

> It troubled him to get used to the world one way and then suddenly have it turn different. He wasn't ready to think of colored people in post offices yet. (39).

The men didn't like the idea that Jody is colored and is expanding the area. He is so used to white people putting them down that he can't understand someone who breaks that mold.

Jody gives his plants to build a store and post-office. He organizes and leads a meeting. He tells everyone to bring axes to help build the roads. He then assigns **Tony Taylor** and Coker to build the buildings. (41).

> Ladies and gent'men, we'se come tuhgether and gethered heah tuh welcome tuh our midst one who has seen fit tuh cast in his lot amongst us. He didn't just come hisself neither. … dat is his wife amongst us also. She couldn't look no mo' better and no nobler if she wuz de queen uh England. -Tony (41)

Jody quickly makes back the money on his land selling it to new families due to the new store. The whole town celebrates what he has built and names him mayor (43). Tony asks for Janie to give a speech, but Jody declines saying she is "uh woman and her place is in de home" (43). Janie didn't complain but was upset at the way he didn't give her a chance.

Jody calls another meeting to build a streetlamp. People didn't really know if they needed one but Jody built up so much hype around it that they thought it was amazing. (44).

> A feeling of coldness and fear took hold of her. She felt far away from things and lonely. (46).

That is sad foreshadowing. Jody is so involved in his projects for the town that he doesn't spend time with Janie and she feels left out from helping.

Since Jody keeps Janie in the house, she doesn't get to talk to people and people start to wonder why they got married. Janie, like the town, feels scared of Jody's strong character. (50).

## Chapter 7

It is described how as the years passed, Janie learns how to internalize all of her thoughts and keep everything "beneath the surface" (76).

> Then one day she sat and watched the shadow of herself going about tending store and prostrating itself before Jody, while all the time she herself sat under a shady tree with the wind blowing through her hair and her clothes. (77).

It is as if she is watching her life from a 3rd person perspective. From the outside she sees herself doing the motions without actually living life.

Janie notices that Jody is getting older. Janie also remembers how Jody would comment that he didn't like it was as if Janie was staying young forever while he grew old (*Dorian reference?!*).

> The more people in there the more ridicule he poured over her body to point attention away from his own …  Don't stand dere rollin' yo' pop eyes at me wid yo' rump hangin' nearly to yo' knees! (78).

I feel so bad for Janie for living with this man for so many years, he is quite horrible brining her down like that to make himself feel better.

> Janie had robbed him of his illusion of irresistible maleness that all men cherish, which was terrible. (79) So he struck Janie with all his might and drove her from the store (80).

I doubt they will stay together after this. But I worry about what Janie will do now, but I am still proud of her for doing what must be done and not subjecting herself to that many years so of torcher.
